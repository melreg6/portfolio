Project Summary

Completed as part of PY 536: Quantum Computing, this project evaluated whether quantum computing will enable the discovery of a new pharmaceutical drug within the next decade. The analysis synthesized concepts from quantum algorithms, resource estimation, error correction, and pharmaceutical development timelines.

My Contributions

- Co-authored a technical research paper evaluating the feasibility of quantum-enabled drug discovery
- Analyzed quantum chemistry resource estimates, including qubit requirements and logical gate depth
- Evaluated the overhead of fault-tolerant quantum error correction
- Compared theoretical simulation capability with realistic hardware projections
- Helped synthesize findings into a structured argument for the in-class debate

In-Class Debate

The project culminated in a 30–40 minute structured debate. My team argued the “Against” position, concluding that although quantum computing holds long-term promise, existing hardware constraints and fault-tolerance requirements make drug-scale simulation unlikely within the next decade.

Our side won the debate by a vote of 6–3.

Key Findings

- Chemically accurate simulation requires on the order of 10¹¹–10¹³ logical gates
- Practical implementations would need approximately 10⁵–10⁶ physical qubits
- Current systems are multiple orders of magnitude smaller
- Drug development already requires 10–15 years in practice
- A quantum-discovered drug within the next decade is therefore unlikely

Contributions

This debate project was completed in partnership with Paul Case for our Quantum Computing course. Paul developed and presented the “For” argument, while I developed and presented the “Against” position. We collaborated equally on the research, technical writing, and development of the presentation slides and final paper.

Reflection

This project strengthened my ability to synthesize technical research, evaluate engineering feasibility, and communicate complex scientific concepts in an academic context. It also provided experience comparing theoretical potential with real-world technological constraints.
